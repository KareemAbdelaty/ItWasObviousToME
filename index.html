Why a game.

Each level is a short fictional scenario inspired by something that happened to me at work, with the real story right after. The aim is to make the lesson land harder by putting you, briefly, in the position of the person on the receiving end of an engineering decision.

I made this a game partly because of Dumb Ways to Die, and partly because about ten years ago I worked for a company that gamified high-school subjects. In my experience, and I do believe this, gamification helps a person internalise a lesson far faster than plain reading. So the hope is that living these moments, rather than reading about them, makes them stick, for you and for me.

TASK 1
WHAT THAT WAS
Design for Intent vs Actual Use

This level was inspired by a real usability test I ran on a medical device. It had a large button you pressed to act and a small indicator light that only reported status. During the session, a participant who was asked to press the button reached for the indicator light instead. The two were not really as confusing as I made them here; I exaggerated that to make the point clearer. To me, the person who designed it, they were obviously different things. To a first-time user, under a bit of pressure, they were both just green features on a panel.

This is what the course calls design for intent versus actual use: I designed each control with a clear intent, but a user does not always act on that intent, and the gap is a foreseeable use error. The way you catch it is by watching real use rather than trusting your own sense of what is clear. You cannot anticipate every mistake at the desk; you find them by putting the design in front of the people who will actually hold it.

[1] R. Razak, "Unintended consequences in design," UX Collective, 2019.  [2] U.S. FDA, Applying Human Factors and Usability Engineering to Medical Devices, 2016 — many use errors cannot be anticipated until real use is observed.

WHAT THAT WAS
Value-Based Engineering

This level also came from a medical device project. I joined as a designer after the initial design phase was done. The device worked and met every requirement in our requirements document. But while testing it, I realized it did not record how much time had passed since it was activated. Nobody had dropped that requirement; nobody had thought of it, so it was never written down. And the elapsed time was medically relevant. The closest example I can point to is a tourniquet: by the time it comes off, the tissue past it may already have gone too long without blood, so whoever takes over needs to know how long it has been on. My device was different, but the principle was the same, the elapsed time was something a person downstream would depend on, and it was not being captured.

Here was the hard part. I was already behind on the work for the next phase, and raising this meant flagging it, arguing for it, and folding in the change, which would put me further behind. It would have been easier to say nothing. I raised it anyway, with the technical lead and the project manager. Only then did it become a requirement, and we added the time recording. This is what the course covered as Value-Based Engineering: the idea that the values you care about, here, safe downstream care for a patient, only protect anyone once they are translated into an actual engineering requirement. The value was never in dispute; the gap was that nobody had turned it into something the system was built to do until I named it.

[3] S. Spiekermann and T. Winkler, "Value-Based Engineering With IEEE 7000," IEEE Technology and Society Magazine, 2022.

WHAT THAT WAS
Professional accountability

Thankfully the real story behind this was much lower risk than a live bench. But one of the things I have learned in engineering is that you have to be precise with instructions, because people follow them as written, not as you pictured them. Here the checklist said power on, confirm the indicator, replace the fuse, and never said power off first, so following it exactly put your hand on something live. I dramatised the danger to make it land. I build test tools other people use, and the setup is so automatic in my head that I forget to say the obvious step, and someone else is the one who loses time, or worse, for my leaving it out.

When a user makes an understandable mistake while following instructions I wrote, the first thing I need to ask is whether the tool or the instructions failed to make something clear, before assuming the person was careless. That is what professional accountability means to me here. APEGA frames protecting public safety as a duty that holds regardless of schedule or convenience. Most days that duty is small and quiet: write down the step that is obvious to you, so it is obvious to them too.

[4] N. G. Leveson, Engineering a Safer World: Systems Thinking Applied to Safety, MIT Press, 2012.  [5] APEGA, Code of Ethics: Five Rules of Conduct Reference Guide, 2023.
In hindsight, and going forward

Thinking like the end user.

Thanks for playing. I hope it was useful, and that putting you in the user's position for a few minutes makes the habit easier to keep: stopping to ask how the person on the other end will actually read a design, not just whether it works the way I intended.

Across the three levels, the lessons I am trying to internalize, and hope you take something from too, are:

  • watch how people really use a design, instead of trusting that it is obvious because it is obvious to me;
  • ask what the requirements quietly left out, not just whether the written ones are met;
  • raise a safety concern even when it is inconvenient or puts me behind;
  • be precise with instructions, because people follow what I actually say, not what I pictured;
  • look at the whole system, so I notice a small problem early enough to act on it.

None of this replaces getting the engineering right. It sits on top of it. Technical correctness is still the centre of the work; I just no longer think it is the whole of it.
